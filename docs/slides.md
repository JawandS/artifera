## Slide 2: Problem & Audience

We're building for professors, especially those in under-resourced institutions.

Anchor stat: 47% of academic institutions in high-income countries have implemented AI tools vs. just 8% in low-income countries. (UNESCO-aligned review, ChemRxiv, 2025)

Three things making this worse:

**No shared space.** Good AI-powered course materials exist, but they stay stuck in individual classrooms. There's no trusted place to find what's actually working.

**No reason to share.** Professors are stretched thin. Without real incentives (money or recognition) the effort of packaging and publishing their work just doesn't happen.

**The gap keeps growing.** Well-resourced universities move fast on AI. Everyone else falls further behind. The professors who could benefit most from shared tools are the ones with the least time and support to find them.

AI is moving fast and most educators feel like they're falling behind. They don't need generic advice but concrete solutions built by their peers.

---

## Slide 3: Solution

Artifera is a free, peer-run library where professors share AI learning experiences and get rewarded for it with a social network that encourages orgnic information sharing.

Three things working together:
- **Library**: real artifacts, organized by subject, course level, and tool, verified by the professors who built them
- **Social**: discussion under every artifact, peers helping each other adapt things to their context
- **Incentives**: grants for top contributors, hackathon prizes from sponsors, and a randomized usage draw so anyone anywhere can earn

Selling platforms like TPT are paywalled, have no real community, and weren't designed for AI-era teaching. Teacher networks have the conversation but nothing you can actually run in class next week. Artifera has both.

---

## Slide 4: What Is an Artifact?

Something concrete. Not "how to use AI in your classroom" advice. An actual thing you can open on Monday and use.

Four examples:
- 📓 Curated NotebookLMs with high-quality sources and guided prompts built around a specific topic
- 📋 A course module with the activity, rubric, and worked examples already written
- 🛠️ 3rd party tools, verified by professors to be effective
- 🔗 Custom vibe-coded tools hosted by Artifera  

Every artifact has a subject, course level, and tool type tagged. It shows how many times it's been used and what other professors said about it. There's a a clear way to get started using the artifact.

Many professors don't have time to redesign their curriculum with every change in AI but artifacts are ready-made, peer-reviewed, and easy to adapt. They're the building blocks for AI-powered learning.

## Slide 6: How Professors Earn

The problem with most reward systems is that they pay the people who are already winning. We built ours differently.

**Performance grants**: top artifacts by verified usage and peer reviews earn quarterly grants. This is how we reward the professors who put in the work to share something good.

**Hackathon prizes**: sponsors run challenge tracks on the platform. Professors build artifacts around a specific tool or theme and compete for prize money. AI companies fund it because they want real educators building real things with their products.

**Random usage draw**: every verified use of an artifact is one entry. A professor in Lagos has the same shot as one at MIT. This is the equity mechanism.

To enter the draw, a professor has to submit a photo of the artifact in use, a short review, and confirmation it ran in a real course session. We verify accounts with institution authentication and 2FA before any of this is accessible. If submissions from an institution look suspicious, the whole institution loses access: which gives universities a real reason to make sure their people aren't gaming it.

The draw also does something else: it gives universities a concrete reason to push AI adoption. More professors using artifacts means more draw entries means more chances for the institution to win funding. It works where top-down mandates don't.

It's not all money either. Top contributors, top institutions, and top regions get featured on leaderboards and in platform spotlights. For a lot of professors, recognition from peers carries real weight.

## Slide 7: How We're Funded

**Education nonprofit grants** pay into the classroom draw pool. Winnings go directly to the professor's institution.

**AI company sponsorships** fund the hackathon prize tracks. Sponsors get professors building real use cases with their tools. We get a high-quality content pipeline for the library.

**Training data licensing (opt-in)** is the third stream. Professors who choose to share their artifact usage data with AI companies get a bonus on their performance grants. It's opt-in only. That means the professors participating actually want to, the data is better quality, and there's no backlash. Everyone wins.

## Slide 8: Roadmap

**Phase 1: Seeding**

Start with an internal hackathon at partner institutions to build the initial user based of content and users. Universities get to advance their AI curriculum in a collaborative fashion. 

**Phase 2: Expand**

First external sponsor, first public hackathon, platform open to other universities. We use boosted draw rounds to drive early adoption.

**Phase 3: Scale**

Focus on partnering with larger organizations (such as a ministries of education). AI-powered recommendations that surface the right artifact for your course without you having to search for it.
