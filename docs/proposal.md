# ArtifactHub: A Global Marketplace for AI-Powered Learning

**ECM x WMGIC International Challenge 2026 — Track 1: AI's Future in Higher Education**

---

## The Problem

Teachers are the single biggest lever in student learning outcomes, but they are time-starved and under-resourced. Most teachers lack the time to research, build, or evaluate new learning materials, and there is no reliable way to share what works across classrooms, schools, or borders. The result is that high-quality, AI-powered learning experiences exist in isolated pockets while most students, particularly those in under-resourced institutions and developing countries, never benefit from them.

Two problems compound this:

1. **Supply:** Teachers with the skills and time to build great materials have no incentive to share them.
2. **Distribution:** Teachers who want better materials have no trusted, curated place to find them.

---

## The Solution: ArtifactHub

ArtifactHub is a teacher-verified marketplace for self-contained learning experiences, called artifacts, powered by a reward system that incentivizes both creation and adoption.

An artifact is any self-contained learning experience a teacher can use directly in the classroom. Examples include:

- A NotebookLM notebook with curated sources and guided prompts
- A lesson plan with activity, rubric, and worked examples
- A vibe-coded interactive tool hosted externally
- A curated link to a third-party solution with verified classroom results

Teachers upload, discover, and use artifacts. The platform rewards the best contributors and actively pulls under-resourced classrooms into the ecosystem through a randomized grant mechanism.

---

## How It Works

### For Teachers Uploading Artifacts
- Create a verified teacher account (school district registration + 2FA)
- Upload an artifact with a description, subject, and grade level
- Earn performance rewards if your artifact gains high engagement, verified usage, and strong reviews

### For Teachers Using Artifacts
- Browse and filter the marketplace by subject, grade, tool type, or rating
- After using an artifact in class, submit a short proof of usage: one photo of the artifact in use (no student faces required), 2-3 sentences on how it was used, and a rating
- Every verified usage submission enters the teacher into a randomized grant drawing

### Reward Structure
Teachers can earn in three ways:

| Path | Reward Type | Who Benefits |
|---|---|---|
| Top-performing artifact | Performance grant | Teacher + school/district |
| Hackathon winner | Sponsor prize | Teacher (per sponsor rules) |
| Random usage selection | Classroom grant | School/district |

The randomized classroom grant is intentional. Popularity-based-only rewards would advantage already well-resourced schools. The random draw ensures any verified teacher anywhere in the world has a real shot at funding, regardless of how many followers they have.

---

## The Hackathon Layer

On top of the core marketplace, ArtifactHub hosts sponsored hackathons where teachers and developers compete to build artifacts around a specific challenge or tool. This layer serves three parties simultaneously:

- **Teachers** get prize money and recognition
- **Sponsors** (Google, Anthropic, OpenAI, etc.) get qualified educators engaging deeply with their products and building real use cases
- **The platform** gets a high-quality content pipeline that feeds directly into the marketplace

Hackathon artifacts become permanent marketplace entries. Other teachers can use them, review them, and enter the grant drawing through normal verified usage. Sponsors can negotiate access to training data from the artifacts produced in their hackathon at a reduced rate, or receive all artifacts from their sponsored event automatically.

---

## Funding Model

| Source | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Education nonprofits | Grants that fund the classroom draw pool |
| AI company sponsors | Hackathon sponsorship fees |
| Training data licensing | Negotiated access to artifact usage data (paid opt-out available for teachers) |

---

## Trust and Safety

- All teachers must register with a verified school district and use 2FA
- Proof of usage is self-reported but deterred by clear consequences: abuse results in district-level bans
- Conflict of interest in hackathon judging is minimized through sponsor-independent review panels
- Student privacy is protected by requiring only product-in-use photos, never student images

---

## Go-to-Market

**Phase 1: Pilot**
Partner with a small group of willing faculty (starting with William and Mary) to seed the platform with quality artifacts. Run one small internal hackathon to validate the model.

**Phase 2: Expand**
Bring in one or two AI company sponsors for a public hackathon. Use boosted grant drawings early on to drive adoption before the artifact library reaches critical mass.

**Phase 3: Scale**
Open to K-12 teachers and international institutions, with a focus on partnerships in developing countries where the classroom grant model has the highest impact.
